Summary

This week lecture introduces the basic concepts of remote sensing and we set up workflows for work with Sentinel-2 and Landsat imagery in QGIS, SNAP and R software.

I learn about different satellite sensors such as Sentinel-2 MSI and Landsat 8/9 OLI, which have varying spatial, spectral, temporal and radiometric resolutions, and why these trade-offs is important when choose data for study cities or environmental changes. For example, Sentinel-2 have 10m spatial resolution for some bands but Landsat is 30m, and this affects what detail we can see in urban areas.

In practical session, we download Sentinel-2 Level-2A surface reflectance data for a select city, locate the 10m bands, and create our own true-colour composite by merging bottom-of-atmosphere (BOA) bands into multiband GeoTIFF file using QGIS, instead of use the pre-pack TCI image with 0-255 values. This shows me how to control display and processing step by step.

Then we move to SNAP, which is sensor-aware GIS tool, load Sentinel product direct from zip file, experiment with band combinations like true colour, false colour, atmospheric penetration and SWIR composites to see different features. We also do resampling from 10m to 20m, masking with vector polygons for specific areas, and generate simple image statistics plus scatterplots for understand spectral feature space. After that, export masked subsets of Sentinel and Landsat data plus shapefiles for land-cover classes like bare earth, grass, forest, low-urban and high-urban to R.

Using terra package, we extract pixel values by class with terra::extract, tidy data with tidyverse, calculate band-wise means and standard deviations per sensor and class. Plot spectral profiles with error bars, density plots, and even t-test on urban reflectance between sensors make abstract idea of electromagnetic radiation, scattering and spectral signatures more concrete.

I have question: is 10m resolution enough for small urban green spaces or informal settlements? Benefit of this is free global data archive with good temporal coverage, but limitation include cloud cover in optical imagery and need for careful resampling to avoid mix pixel values. Overall, practical link theory to hands-on analysis well.

Applications

Remote sensing data from Landsat and Sentinel-2 is widely used in urban and environmental research plus policy making, because provide multi-temporal free archives for track changes over large areas. Landsat with 30m resolution and 16-day revisit, Sentinel-2 at 10-20m and 5-day revisit support study like forest loss, illegal logging, urban green-space mapping, flood response and urban heat island effect.

Researcher create spectral indices such NDVI for vegetation health, NDBI for built-up areas, and others from spectral index database, which feed into global product like Dynamic World for near-real-time 10m land-cover classification. This approach vital for policy, allow cities and government monitor progress on Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), climate mitigation plan and disaster risk framework without rely on slow expensive ground surveys or administrative data.

For instance, consistent calibrated measure of vegetation greenness and surface moisture inform drought early-warning system or evaluate if afforestation, wetland restoration project actually visible in landscape over time. In urban context, these method help assess green infrastructure effectiveness or track impervious surface growth relate to flood risk.

More advance technique from practical like Tasseled Cap transformation and principal component analysis (PCA)-style reduction connect direct to peer-review literature on urban classification and change detection. Tasseled Cap component—brightness, greenness, wetness—original for Landsat but adaptable to Sentinel-2 when bands match spectrally, give compact indices that separate urban fabric, vegetation and water or bare soil more cleanly than raw bands. This widely apply to map urban expansion, identify high-biomass vs low-biomass area, detect moisture change for metropolitan climate strategy or risk mapping.

Lecture mention SAR and polarization briefly, which literature use for see through clouds, monitor flood, landslide or urban infrastructure deformation—crucial in tropical or cloudy region where optical data have temporal gap. The full workflow we follow—atmospheric correction to surface reflectance, careful resampling, masking to policy-relevant boundary like administrative unit, then extract and statistically compare spectral signature—mirror structure in many publish EO case study and policy report on urban environmental challenge. Different study take vary approach: some focus supervised classification with machine learning on transform bands, other use time-series analysis for change detect, but all emphasize pre-processing quality for reliable result.

Reflection

This week teaching make me realise that remote sensing not just about download pretty satellite image, but control long chain of decision from atmospheric scattering and surface interaction, through resampling, masking to how summarise and visualise spectral signatures in analysis.

The four resolution types—spatial, temporal, spectral, radiometric—directly limit what urban question we can realistically answer and at what scale, for example fine 10m detail good for park mapping but maybe not individual building in dense city.

It realistic to integrate Earth Observation (EO) into policy like urban planning or environmental governance, especially with cloud platform like Google Earth Engine that handle big data process, but constraint such data download time, require specialist skill, cloud contamination and computational resource could be problem for small government or researcher in develop country.

Personal, I find spectral profile comparison between Sentinel and Landsat most interesting, because show how same land cover like high-urban look different across sensor due to band pass and radiometric resolution—this why important match sensor for time-series study. Also benefit to see error bar from standard deviation, give sense of variability within class that raw image not show.

For my future as urban spatial science student at UCL, this tool and workflow very useful, because I can apply to research on Airbnb impact, short-term rental regulation or commercial real estate analysis by extract spectral signature for urban fabric like dense informal settlement vs high-rise CBD, then link to policy target such SDG 11 sustainable city.

Even if I not use SNAP every time due to prefer Python or R fully, the concept grounding—how sensor work, what spectral signature mean, why resampling and masking matter—will stay with me for design EO-base analysis in master thesis or job at consult like CBRE. Maybe similar tool like ESA SNAP evolve to more user-friend for non-expert policymaker.

Overall, this week spark interest in combine EO with my GIS and data science skill for real-world urban problem.
